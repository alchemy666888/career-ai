# AI Job Search User Guide for Job Hunters

This guide explains how to use AI Job Search to organize your search, choose stronger roles, prepare applications, and track every opportunity from discovery to outcome.

## Who this guide is for

Use this guide if you are actively looking for a job and want one place to manage:

- Roles you discover.
- Jobs you shortlist.
- Applications you are preparing.
- Submissions and follow-ups.
- Interviews and offers.
- Accepted or closed opportunities.

## Key concepts

### Pipeline

The pipeline is your job-search board. Each role belongs to one stage:

| Stage | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Discovered** | You found the role but have not decided whether to pursue it. |
| **Shortlisted** | You saved the role because it is worth deeper review. |
| **Applying** | You are preparing documents and checking requirements. |
| **Submitted** | You submitted externally and marked it submitted in the app. |
| **Interviewing** | You are preparing for or tracking interviews. |
| **Offer** | You received an offer or are discussing final offer details. |
| **Accepted** | You accepted the role. |
| **Closed** | The role is no longer active because it was rejected, withdrawn, expired, declined, or otherwise closed. |

### Shortlist

Your Shortlist is the set of roles you saved because they deserve attention. Use it to avoid losing promising opportunities in a long list of job posts.

### Application workspace

An application workspace is where you prepare for one role. It can include tailored document drafts, notes, and a checklist. The app does not submit applications for you; you submit on the employer site, then mark the role as submitted.

### Evidence and fit

Evidence is information you approve from your profile, résumé, or corrections. Fit evaluation uses that evidence to explain strengths, gaps, and confidence instead of relying on generic advice.

## Recommended workflow

## 1. Start with your profile

Before applying heavily, make sure your profile and résumé information are accurate.

1. Open **Profile**.
2. Add your target roles, preferred locations, skills, and background.
3. If résumé import is available, review extracted claims carefully.
4. Correct any imported claim that is inaccurate or incomplete.
5. Keep your strongest stories and measurable outcomes easy to reuse.

**Tip:** Treat your profile as your source of truth. Better evidence leads to better application and interview preparation.

## 2. Discover roles

1. Open **Discover** or `/jobs`.
2. Search by keyword, role, company, location, or work style.
3. Review the role description, source, freshness, and requirements.
4. Save promising roles.
5. Dismiss obvious mismatches so your list stays focused.

If you already have a job description from another site, use manual import when available. Paste the job title, company, location, work style, canonical URL if available, and job description text.

## 3. Decide what to shortlist

Shortlist a role when most of these are true:

- The role matches your target title or a realistic adjacent title.
- The work style and location are acceptable.
- The responsibilities are interesting.
- You can show evidence for the most important requirements.
- The compensation, level, or growth path seems worth exploring.
- You can apply before the deadline.

Use notes to capture:

- Why the role is attractive.
- Concerns or deal breakers.
- Missing information to research.
- A next action and deadline.

## 4. Prepare an application

When you are ready to apply:

1. Open the shortlisted role.
2. Start or open its application workspace.
3. Tailor your résumé to the job requirements.
4. Draft or revise a cover letter if needed.
5. Complete the preflight checklist.
6. Submit on the employer site or job board.
7. Return to AI Job Search and mark the application as **Submitted**.

### Application preflight checklist

Before submitting, confirm:

- Your name and contact details are correct.
- Your résumé is targeted to the role.
- Your cover letter, if used, names the right company and role.
- Links to portfolio, LinkedIn, GitHub, or website work.
- Required questions are answered truthfully.
- No unsupported claim was added by mistake.
- You saved a copy of what you submitted.

## 5. Follow up after submission

After a role enters **Submitted**:

- Record the submission date.
- Add the application portal or confirmation information to notes.
- Set a follow-up reminder based on the employer's process.
- Keep preparing for likely screening questions.

A simple follow-up note can include:

```text
Submitted on: YYYY-MM-DD
Source: Company careers page / referral / job board
Confirmation: Email received / portal status / none
Follow-up target: YYYY-MM-DD
Next action: Prepare recruiter screen examples
```

## 6. Prepare for interviews

When a role moves to **Interviewing**:

1. Review the job description again.
2. Identify the top responsibilities and requirements.
3. Choose stories that prove relevant experience.
4. Practice answers for behavioral and technical questions.
5. Prepare questions for the interviewer.
6. Record signals after each conversation.

### Interview story format

Use this structure:

- **Situation:** What was happening?
- **Task:** What were you responsible for?
- **Action:** What did you personally do?
- **Result:** What changed? Use numbers when possible.
- **Relevance:** Why does it matter for this role?

## 7. Manage offers

When a role reaches **Offer**:

- Compare compensation, benefits, work style, location, manager fit, team health, learning potential, and risk.
- Write down questions before negotiation.
- Track deadlines and decision constraints.
- Avoid closing other strong opportunities until you are confident in the final offer.

Move the role to **Accepted** only after you have formally accepted. Move other opportunities to **Closed** with a note explaining why.

## 8. Close roles cleanly

Use **Closed** when:

- The employer rejected the application.
- You withdrew.
- The role expired.
- You declined an offer.
- The role no longer fits your search.

Good closure notes help you learn from the search. Record the reason, any useful feedback, and whether you would consider the employer later.

## Practical weekly routine

### Daily, 15–30 minutes

- Review new discovered roles.
- Save or dismiss roles quickly.
- Update notes on active applications.
- Respond to interview or recruiter activity.

### Twice per week, 45–90 minutes

- Prepare and submit selected applications.
- Tailor documents for high-fit roles.
- Follow up on submitted applications.

### Weekly review, 30 minutes

- Count roles by pipeline stage.
- Remove stale roles.
- Identify bottlenecks.
- Decide which roles deserve deep work next week.

## Tips for better outcomes

- Apply selectively instead of sending generic applications everywhere.
- Keep your Shortlist small enough to act on.
- Write notes immediately after interviews.
- Use evidence and specific examples in every document.
- Do not mark an application submitted until it is actually submitted externally.
- Keep closed opportunities for learning, not clutter.

## Privacy and control reminders

- Review all generated or suggested text before using it.
- Do not include sensitive information unless necessary.
- The app is a preparation and tracking tool, not a promise of employment.
- You remain responsible for external submissions, employer communications, and final decisions.
